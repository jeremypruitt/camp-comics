# Card-deck review surface supersedes the single-head-card stack

The single-head-card review surface from ADR-0009 (slices C, D, E, F, G, H, I, K) is replaced by a true **card-deck** visualization: from the moment the operator enters the review surface, all twelve [[Review unit]]s of the comic (panel 1, panel 2, the panels-3–5 triptych, panels 6–11, the panels-12–14 triptych, panel 15, and the cover) appear physically stacked as cards. The top card is large and prominent, with one or two cards peeking behind for stack feel. The operator works through the deck with **swipe LEFT to accept, swipe RIGHT to re-roll, swipe UP/DOWN to cycle candidates** — inverted from ADR-0009's right-accept/left-reroll. Long-press Re-prompt from ADR-0009 slice F is dropped from the active surface (the `PromptBuilder` `addendum:` arg lands as harmless dead code, preserved for a possible later return). Accept animates the top card off the top of the stack, revealing the next. The persistent **Finalize** button in the toolbar lets the operator render-to-PDF anytime, with empty cells handled by slice H's existing PDF-render path.

The motivation is dominated by the field reality of the ADR-0009 device-verify session: the surface was unusable end-to-end (Jeremy burned 29/32 of the per-comic budget without completing a single comic, see #90), and the root cause was not gesture choice — it was the *single-head-card* framing. By hiding the deck behind one focused card with a rigid "Panel 6 of 15" label, ADR-0009 made every navigation a confirm-and-jump operation (slices I + #89), made every failure a separate UI surface to dismiss (slice H + #92), and made the operator feel like they were grinding through a queue rather than working a deck. The card-deck framing collapses navigation, failure presentation, and progress sensing into the deck itself: progress is the discard pile's growth; navigation is the deck position; failure is just a card you swipe past. Jeremy's framing during the redesign grill was direct: *"the swipe design is right and is world famous in apps that use them. Tinder works great. Your solution breaks in many ways."* See [[project-swipe-stack-vision-2026-05-31]] for the source vision.

**The placeholder IS the panel frame.** When a card is in flight (queue hasn't returned a candidate yet), the card renders the actual panel shape that will appear in the final comic — same dimensions, same border, same caption from the YAML already visible — with the image area empty/spinning inside. There is no separate dark "generating" UI. For triptychs, the composed P-in (`/` diagonal) or H-out (parallel-edge) frame is visible from the start with three independent image slots, each spinning until its sub-panel candidate lands. This serves two purposes: the operator sees what's coming (the placeholder previews the destination), and the wait time feels like progress rather than uncertainty (a partial triptych with two slots filled and one spinning is visibly *almost done*, where ADR-0009's "wait for all 3, then snap" was visibly stuck). The caption already being visible gives the operator something to read during the wait — generation latency stops being dead time.

**Panel 1 still serializes before panels 2–15 + cover fan out.** All twelve cards appear in the deck from t=0 as panel-shaped placeholders. Panel 1's card is the top card and generates first; panels 2–15 + cover sit as placeholders with their captions visible. The moment panel 1 has *any* candidate on disk (not necessarily accepted), the [[Generation queue]] enqueues the remaining eleven units and the worker pool starts fanning out. The operator cannot swipe-accept past panel 1 until panel 1 has a candidate; this is naturally enforced because the top card has nothing to accept yet. Panel-1 latency (~5–10s for one Gemini call) is short enough that by the time the operator has read panel 1's caption and oriented themselves, the candidate has typically landed. This preserves ADR-0009's continuity invariant (panels 2–15 + cover anchor on `panel_01.png`) while hiding the Phase 1 / Phase 2 split entirely from the operator — the deck appears unified from the start. The atomic-triptych invariant from ADR-0009 slice G also survives intact: a triptych is one card in the deck, Accept writes 3 PNGs atomically, Re-roll regenerates all 3 (spends 3 budget), Up/Down cycles the *whole composed triptych* (the operator picks the best composed take, not the best of each sub-panel).

**Budget exhaustion is a soft-block, not a hard wall.** When `GenerationBudget.remaining == 0`, the budget chip flips to a persistent "0 — accept-only" indicator. Swipe-left (accept) and swipe-up/down (cycle existing candidates, zero API calls) continue to work; swipe-right (re-roll) silently bounces with a brief haptic. The operator can finish the comic by accepting whatever they have. No modal sheet, no forced-decision dialog — the surface remains calm. This addresses #90's structural blocker: the prior failure mode was burning budget on accidental re-rolls (slice I's tap-to-confirm dialogs, the failed-card Retry surface, repeated tapping on triptych "Generating…" hangs). The card-deck surface closes all those accidental-spend paths by construction (no grid-tap-to-confirm, no failed-card Retry button, no triptych-hang-with-no-feedback). Plus a **per-card re-roll soft cap with friction**: after three re-rolls on the same card, a fourth swipe-right shows a one-question confirm — "this is your fourth re-roll on this panel; sure?". The confirm is the entire UX of the cap; there's no hard ceiling. This protects against runaway burn on one stubborn panel without restricting normal creative iteration.

**The Finalize button is persistent and the deck has no forced "done" state.** A Finalize toolbar button is always available next to the grid icon. Tapping it triggers PDF render with whatever is accepted at that moment; un-accepted panels render as empty cells per slice H. There is no auto-present-at-completion (the ADR-0009 slice I behaviour that was driving false-positive Finalize prompts under the "is this terminal?" check); the operator decides when they are done. The deck simply runs out of cards naturally when the operator has worked through it, and the empty-deck state shows a quiet "all cards reviewed — Finalize from the toolbar" prompt. **Truly stuck cards** (whose generation has failed after the queue's 7-attempt exponential-backoff retry budget, per PR #91) stay in their deck position as a distinctly visualized "couldn't generate" card — the panel frame is visible but greyed out, with a single small "tap to try one more time" affordance that fires one manual generation against the budget. Swipe-left on a stuck card accepts nothing and moves past (leaving an empty cell the PDF handles); swipe-right is disabled (there's no candidate to roll from). This preserves the deck's mental model ("a card exists for each panel") while giving the operator visibility into what failed and one manual escape hatch.

**The grid is the only undo path.** ADR-0009 slice I's grid view survives but its interaction model changes wholesale: tapping any grid cell **pulls that card back to the top of the deck**. An accepted card's PNG is demoted back to a candidate in `_candidates/0/`; the deck position re-pops that card to the top, where the operator can swipe-right to re-roll (now spending budget for the new generation), swipe-left to re-accept the existing candidate, or swipe-up/down to see if other candidates exist. The grid itself is read-only otherwise — no auto-present, no confirm dialogs, no tap-fires-generation behaviour. This is the entire undo affordance for the surface; there is no Tinder-style rewind button on the deck, no per-card history navigation. The grid icon in the toolbar is always one tap away, and the deck remains uncluttered. This obsoletes the entire `pendingAcceptedDemoteID` / `pendingDeferredRetryID` / `performAcceptedDemote` / `performDeferredRetry` complex from slice I — those were the right *semantic* (demote-then-jump) hidden behind the wrong *gate* (a confirm dialog firing before the jump happened, leaving the dialog rendered over stale content; see #89).

**Entry path collapses: drop the Start CTA.** `PlayerDetailView`'s Continue button now navigates directly into the deck — there is no intermediate Start screen. The Start CTA from ADR-0009 slice C was meaningful when it represented the operator's commitment to spend a generation on panel 1 with Phase 2 fanning out afterward; with the deck visualization the deck IS the commitment surface (the operator sees twelve cards generating). A separate Start screen would be ceremony adding nothing. Sponsored-trial spend (`SponsoredTrialBackend.spend(playerId:)` from slice C) moves to first-deck-appearance for that player: the spend fires on the first time `ReviewDeckView` mounts for a given player, idempotent per-player. The `ReviewStackView` intermediate Phase-1-then-Phase-2 view from slice C is deleted; the deck view is the entry point. Onboarding overlay (slice K) updates its `OverlayHint` cases to match the inverted gestures and drops the `.reprompt` hint (since long-press is gone); the ghost-finger animation re-summon path from Settings is unchanged.

**Surface code that survives this redesign.** `GenerationQueue` (slice D + PR #91 auto-retry) is the only major piece preserved intact — its actor + adaptive K + ordered emission + exponential-backoff retry are correct for the new surface. `PanelTriptych` + `ReviewUnit` (slice G) preserve the triptych-as-one-unit semantics. `PanelGridView` (slice 11d) renders the grid. `PhotoReferenceResolver` (slice B) handles continuity. `GenerationBudget` (slice A) handles budget arithmetic. The dynamic-budget formula `(panel_count + 1) × 2` from ADR-0009 stays — re-rolls are gated by the soft-block + per-card cap above rather than by formula adjustment. Slice F's `PromptBuilder.addendum:` arg is kept as dead code (harmless additive parameter); the long-press sheet UI and `RepromptAddendumSheet` are deleted. `Phase2StackView` is deleted wholesale; the new `ReviewDeckView` takes its place with a much smaller surface area (no failed-card UI, no Start handoff, no head-only switching).

**What we are explicitly choosing not to redo.** The atomic-triptych invariant (slice G), the panel-1 anchor (slice B), the dynamic budget formula (slice A), the friendly-error helper + queue auto-retry (PR #91), the on-disk player-state model (slices 5, 6), the YAML 15-panel arc (slices 16, 17), the bundled-template + class-key indirection — all of these were correct decisions that survive. ADR-0009 is therefore **partially superseded by this ADR**: the slice-C Start CTA, the slice-D Phase 2 worker-pool host (`Phase2StackView`), the slice-F long-press Re-prompt UI, the slice-H failed-card head surface, the slice-I confirm-dialog grid-tap path, and the slice-K overlay's `.reprompt` case are all replaced by the card-deck design above. ADR-0009's underlying primitives are kept and reused. The supersede notice on ADR-0009 should be amended to point here.

The single sharp edge introduced: in the card-deck framing, an operator who accepts a card and immediately regrets it has to route through the grid (one tap on the toolbar grid icon, then one tap on the cell) to pull it back. This is two taps where Tinder+ provides a rewind button (one tap). The trade-off was explicit: keep the deck uncluttered with zero recovery UI on the deck surface itself; trust the grid icon to be discoverable and fast. If field experience shows the grid recovery path is too slow in practice, a small "rewind last accept" affordance can be added later without disturbing the rest of the design.
